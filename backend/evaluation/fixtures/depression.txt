Therapist: Hi Marcus, thanks for coming in today. How has your week been?

Client: Honestly... I almost didn't come. I sat in the parking lot for like ten minutes trying to make myself get out of the car.

Therapist: I appreciate you telling me that, and I'm glad you made it in. What was going through your mind in the parking lot?

Client: Just... what's the point. I don't know if this is helping. I don't know if anything is helping.

Therapist: That feeling of "what's the point" — has that been showing up a lot lately?

Client: Pretty much every day. I wake up and I just... don't want to do anything. Getting out of bed feels like climbing a mountain. I hit snooze like five or six times every morning.

Therapist: How has your sleep been overall?

Client: I sleep too much, actually. I'll sleep ten, eleven hours and still feel exhausted. On weekends I sometimes don't get up until the afternoon. And then I feel guilty about wasting the day, which makes me feel worse.

Therapist: So there's a cycle there — low energy leads to oversleeping, which leads to guilt, which drains your energy further.

Client: Yeah, exactly. It's like this loop I can't get out of.

Therapist: When did you first notice this pattern starting?

Client: Maybe... three months ago? It got worse gradually. At first I just thought I was tired from work, but then I stopped caring about things I used to enjoy. Like, I used to play basketball with my friends every Saturday morning. Haven't gone in over a month.

Therapist: Have your friends reached out about that?

Client: Yeah, my buddy Derek texts me every Friday asking if I'm coming. I usually just say I'm busy or I'm not feeling well. Last week I didn't even respond.

Therapist: How did that feel, not responding?

Client: Bad. But also... easier than coming up with an excuse. I know that sounds terrible.

Therapist: It doesn't sound terrible. It sounds like withdrawing feels protective in the moment, even though it creates more distance over time.

Client: I guess so. It's like... being around people takes so much energy. Everyone is all cheerful and I'm just... not. I feel like I'm bringing everyone down.

Therapist: Is that something you know for sure, or something you're assuming?

Client: I mean... nobody's said that to me. But I can feel it. Or I think I can feel it.

Therapist: I want to explore that a little. You mentioned Derek texts you every week. What does that suggest about how he feels about having you around?

Client: ...That he wants me there, I guess. But he's just being nice.

Therapist: Maybe. Or maybe he genuinely misses having you there. Both are possible, right?

Client: Yeah. I just default to the negative interpretation.

Therapist: That's a really honest observation. Depression has a way of putting a filter on everything — it makes the negative feel true and the positive feel like an exception. Have you noticed that filter in other areas?

Client: For sure. At work, if I do nine things right and one thing wrong, I only think about the one thing wrong. My boss complimented a report I wrote last week and I immediately thought she was just being polite.

Therapist: So the depression is affecting work too. How has your concentration been?

Client: Terrible. I stare at my screen for hours and barely get anything done. Simple tasks take me three times as long. I used to be really efficient and now I feel useless.

Therapist: I want to introduce a concept called behavioral activation. The idea is that when depression lowers our motivation, we tend to stop doing the things that give us energy and connection. And that withdrawal makes the depression worse. So instead of waiting to feel motivated, we start with small actions and let the motivation follow.

Client: Like fake it till you make it?

Therapist: Not exactly. It's more like... you don't wait for the feeling to come first. You do the action, and often the feeling follows. But we start very small. What's something you used to enjoy that feels low-effort?

Client: I used to like cooking. Nothing fancy, just trying new recipes on Sunday afternoons. I haven't done that in weeks. I've been living on takeout and cereal.

Therapist: Okay. What if this week, you picked one simple recipe — something that takes thirty minutes or less — and made it? Not because you feel like it, but as an experiment to see what happens.

Client: I could probably do that. Maybe scrambled eggs with vegetables or something. Nothing complicated.

Therapist: That's perfect. And I want you to rate your mood before you start cooking and after you finish, on a scale of one to ten. We're just gathering data.

Client: Okay. What if I can't make myself do it?

Therapist: Then we talk about what got in the way, and that's useful information too. There's no failing this exercise. I also want to talk about the social withdrawal piece. Responding to Derek's text — how hard does that feel on a scale of one to ten?

Client: Responding? Maybe a five. Actually going to basketball? Like an eight.

Therapist: So let's start with the five. This week, when Derek texts, could you respond — even if it's just "hey, can't make it this week but thanks for asking"?

Client: Yeah, I can do that. That actually doesn't feel too bad.

Therapist: Good. We're not trying to overhaul everything at once. Small steps. Respond to Derek, cook one meal, and rate your mood before and after. That's the whole plan for this week.

Client: That feels doable. I was afraid you were going to tell me to go to basketball and start a whole exercise routine and call all my friends.

Therapist: No. When everything feels heavy, piling on more tasks just confirms the feeling that you can't handle things. We build momentum slowly. Can I also ask — are you eating regular meals?

Client: Not really. Sometimes I skip breakfast and lunch and just eat a big dinner. Or I snack all day and never have a real meal.

Therapist: Irregular eating can really worsen low mood and fatigue. As part of the cooking experiment, could you aim to eat at least two actual meals a day? They don't have to be elaborate.

Client: I'll try. I know you're right. I just don't have the energy to care about feeding myself properly.

Therapist: And that lack of self-care is the depression talking, not a reflection of your character. You mentioned you used to be efficient at work, you played basketball regularly, you cooked for yourself. That person is still there. We're just clearing the fog so you can access that version of yourself again.

Client: ...Yeah. I hope so. Some days it feels like that person is gone for good.

Therapist: I understand why it feels that way. Depression is very convincing. But the fact that you sat in that parking lot for ten minutes and still walked through my door tells me something about your resilience. You showed up even when every part of you was saying don't bother.

Client: I hadn't thought about it like that.

Therapist: So this week — one meal cooked, respond to Derek, and mood ratings before and after activities. We'll review all of it next session and adjust from there. Sound good?

Client: Yeah. Sounds good. Thanks for not making it overwhelming.

Therapist: Of course. Same time next week?

Client: Yeah. I'll be here. Might sit in the parking lot for a bit first, but I'll be here.

Therapist: That counts. See you then, Marcus.
