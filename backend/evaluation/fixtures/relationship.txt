Therapist: Hi Dana, good to see you again. How have things been at home this week?

Client: Awful. We had another blowup on Tuesday night. Over something completely stupid. The dishwasher. We got into a screaming match about who was supposed to unload the dishwasher.

Therapist: It sounds like the dishwasher was the surface issue. What do you think was underneath it?

Client: I mean, it's never really about the dishes, right? It's about the fact that I feel like I do everything around the house and Ryan doesn't notice or care. I work full-time, I handle the kids' schedules, I cook most of the meals, and then he comes home and sits on the couch and watches TV. And when I ask for help, he acts like I'm nagging.

Therapist: How does it feel when he says you're nagging?

Client: Infuriating. Like, I'm not nagging. I'm asking my partner to be a partner. But the word nagging makes me feel like the bad guy for having basic expectations. It shuts down the whole conversation because now I'm defending myself instead of talking about what I actually need.

Therapist: So the word itself derails things. Let's talk about what happened during Tuesday's argument. Walk me through it.

Client: I got home from work around six. The dishwasher was full of clean dishes — I'd run it that morning before I left. Ryan had been home since four. He hadn't touched it. The kids were hungry, and I needed the counter space to make dinner. So I said, "Can you please unload the dishwasher?" And he sighed. This big dramatic sigh like I'd asked him to build a house.

Therapist: And how did you respond to the sigh?

Client: I lost it. I said something like "Oh I'm sorry, is unloading a dishwasher too much to ask?" And then he said I was always on his case, and I said maybe I wouldn't be on his case if he did anything without being asked, and then we were off to the races. Yelling, door slamming, the whole thing. The kids heard everything. That's the part that really gets to me.

Therapist: The kids hearing — that bothers you more than the argument itself?

Client: Way more. My parents fought like that when I was growing up and I swore I'd never do that to my kids. And here I am, repeating the pattern. My daughter looked scared. She's seven. She shouldn't have to hear her parents screaming at each other.

Therapist: That awareness is important, Dana. You're recognizing a pattern you want to break. Let me ask — after the argument, what happened?

Client: We didn't talk for the rest of the night. We do this thing where we just go silent. He watches TV in the living room, I go to the bedroom with my laptop. Eventually we go to bed and lie there in the dark, not speaking. And in the morning we pretend nothing happened.

Therapist: The silent treatment. Does it feel like a resolution or more like a pause?

Client: A pause. The problem never gets solved. It just gets buried until next time. We have the same fight over and over, just with different props. Last month it was about who forgot to pick up the dry cleaning. Before that it was about whose turn it was to take the dog to the vet.

Therapist: It sounds like the recurring theme is about feeling unseen and unsupported. Like the division of labor is unequal and your efforts aren't acknowledged.

Client: Exactly. And I love Ryan. I do. We've been together twelve years and I don't want this to end. But I'm so frustrated all the time. I feel like I'm living with a roommate, not a husband. We don't connect anymore. We don't talk about anything real. It's all logistics and arguments.

Therapist: When was the last time you felt connected to him?

Client: ...God, I have to think about that. Maybe last summer? We went on a trip to the coast, just the two of us. My parents took the kids. And for three days it was like we were ourselves again. We talked, we laughed, we actually enjoyed each other. But as soon as we got home, it was right back to the routine.

Therapist: So the connection is still there when the daily stressors are removed. That's actually a hopeful sign. It means the issue isn't that you've lost the capacity to connect — it's that the patterns you've fallen into at home are blocking it.

Client: I guess. But we can't live at the beach forever.

Therapist: No, but we can look at what was different about the beach. No logistics, no division of labor arguments, undivided attention. The question is how to bring some of that into everyday life. Let me teach you a communication technique that might help with the arguments. It's called an "I" statement. Instead of "Can you please unload the dishwasher" — which is a request but can feel like a directive — you lead with how you feel. For example: "I feel overwhelmed when I come home and there's more to do. I need help with the dishes so I can start dinner."

Client: That feels so... formal. Like I'm reading from a script.

Therapist: It does feel awkward at first. But the goal is to shift from accusation to expression. When Ryan hears "you never do anything," his brain goes into defense mode. When he hears "I feel overwhelmed," there's more room for empathy. Would you be willing to try it once this week?

Client: I can try. But what if he doesn't respond to it? What if he still sighs or brushes me off?

Therapist: That's possible. You can only control your side of the conversation. But often, when one person changes their approach, it shifts the dynamic. If he still brushes you off, we address that. You might say, "When you sigh, I feel dismissed, and it makes me not want to ask for help at all."

Client: ...That's actually what I feel. I just never say it that way. I go straight to anger because the hurt underneath is harder to show.

Therapist: That's a really important insight. Anger often shows up as a bodyguard for more vulnerable feelings — hurt, disappointment, loneliness. Ryan sees the anger and reacts to that, but he never gets to the real feeling underneath because it's hidden behind the frustration.

Client: So I need to be more vulnerable with him. That's terrifying, honestly. What if he doesn't care?

Therapist: That fear makes sense, especially given your history. Being vulnerable means risking disappointment. But right now, the anger approach isn't working either. You're both stuck in a pattern that leaves you both disconnected and frustrated.

Client: True. The current way definitely isn't working.

Therapist: I'd also like to talk about the conflict pattern itself. You mentioned the cycle — argument, silent treatment, pretend it didn't happen, repeat. What if you both agreed on a time-out signal? When things start escalating, either of you can call a pause. Not a silent treatment, but a deliberate, "I need twenty minutes to cool down, and then let's come back to this."

Client: That could work. The problem is that in the moment, I don't want to pause. I want to be heard. Walking away feels like letting him off the hook.

Therapist: I understand that. But are you being heard during the screaming match?

Client: ...No. We're both just yelling over each other.

Therapist: Right. So the argument itself isn't accomplishing the goal of being heard. A pause gives you both time to move from reactive to reflective. And the commitment to come back to the conversation — that's the key part. It's not avoidance. It's a reset.

Client: Okay. I'll talk to him about the time-out thing. He might actually go for it because I know he hates the yelling too. He's told me he shuts down when I raise my voice.

Therapist: That's useful to know. He might be going silent not to punish you but because he's overwhelmed by the intensity. Everyone has a different tolerance for emotional escalation.

Client: I never thought of it from his side like that. I always assumed the silence was passive-aggressive.

Therapist: It might be. Or it might be self-protection. Understanding his experience doesn't mean excusing the behavior, but it might change how you approach it. Have you two ever considered couples counseling?

Client: We've talked about it but never pulled the trigger. He's not great at talking about feelings. He grew up in a family where you just didn't do that.

Therapist: That context is helpful. He may not have the emotional vocabulary yet. Couples counseling could give you both a structured space to practice. In the meantime, let's focus on what you can do. This week, I'd like you to try two things. First, use one "I" statement during a moment of frustration instead of a "you" accusation. Second, propose the time-out idea to Ryan when things are calm — not during a fight.

Client: When things are calm. Okay. Maybe this weekend when the kids are at my parents' house.

Therapist: That's a good time. You could even frame it as something you're learning in therapy that you want to try together. That way it's an invitation, not a criticism.

Client: "My therapist taught me this thing" sounds a lot less combative than "we need to talk about how you never do anything." Point taken.

Therapist: Exactly. How are you feeling about all this?

Client: Better than when I walked in. I'm still frustrated, but I feel like I have something to actually try instead of just being angry and hoping things magically change.

Therapist: Frustration is valid. The goal isn't to stop feeling frustrated — it's to express it in a way that opens a door instead of slamming one. Same time next week?

Client: Yes. And thank you. This is the first time I've felt like there might be a way through this that doesn't end in either screaming or giving up.

Therapist: There usually is. We just have to find it together. See you next week, Dana.
